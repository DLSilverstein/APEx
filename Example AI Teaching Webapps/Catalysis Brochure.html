Catalytic Reactions

The 7 Steps of Heterogeneous Catalysis

Introduction

Heterogeneous Catalysis

In heterogeneous catalysis, the catalyst is in a different phase (usually solid) than the reactants and products (usually gas or liquid). For a reaction to occur, reactant molecules must travel from the bulk fluid to the active sites on the catalyst surface, react, and the products must return to the bulk fluid.

This entire process is traditionally broken down into seven sequential steps. The overall rate of reaction is governed by the slowest of these steps, known as the rate-limiting step (RLS) or rate-determining step (RDS).

The 7 Sequential Steps:

  1. External diffusion of reactant(s) to the catalyst surface.
  2. Internal diffusion of reactant(s) through catalyst pores.
  3. Adsorption of reactant(s) onto the catalytic sites.
  4. Surface reaction on the catalyst surface.
  5. Desorption of product(s) from the surface.
  6. Internal diffusion of product(s) out of the pores.
  7. External diffusion of product(s) into the bulk fluid.
Step 1

External Diffusion of Reactants

Before a reaction can occur, the reactant molecules must travel from the bulk fluid phase across the hydrodynamic boundary layer to the external surface of the catalyst pellet.

This mass transfer is driven by a concentration gradient between the bulk fluid (CA,b) and the external surface of the catalyst (CA,s). The flux of reactant A, WA, can be modeled using a mass transfer coefficient (kc):

WA = kc (CA,b − CA,s)

Key Considerations:

  • Hydrodynamics: The mass transfer coefficient kc is highly dependent on fluid velocity. Increasing the flow rate decreases the boundary layer thickness, thereby increasing kc.
  • Temperature: External diffusion has a weak temperature dependence (usually proportional to T3/2) compared to surface chemical reactions (which follow an exponential Arrhenius relationship).
  • If external diffusion is the slowest step, the measured reaction rate will be heavily influenced by mixing/flow conditions rather than intrinsic catalytic kinetics.
Step 2

Internal Diffusion of Reactants

Most industrial catalysts are highly porous to maximize their surface area. Once the reactant reaches the external surface, it must diffuse through this tortuous pore network to reach the active sites deep inside the pellet.

Internal diffusion is characterized by an effective diffusivity (De), which accounts for the porosity (εp), tortuosity (τ), and constriction factor (σ) of the pores:

De = DAB εp σ τ

The Thiele Modulus & Effectiveness Factor:

To quantify how much internal diffusion limits the reaction, chemical engineers use the Thiele Modulus (φ), which is essentially the ratio of the surface reaction rate to the internal diffusion rate.

The Internal Effectiveness Factor (η) relates the actual overall rate to the rate that would occur if the entire interior were exposed to the external surface concentration:

η = Actual overall rate Rate evaluated at external surface conditions

For a first-order reaction in a spherical pellet, when internal diffusion is highly limiting (large φ), η ≈ 3/φ.

Step 3

Adsorption of Reactants

Once inside the pore, the reactant molecule A must attach to an active site (S) on the catalyst surface to form an adsorbed complex (A·S).

A + S ⇌ A·S

The Langmuir Adsorption Isotherm

The most common model to describe this is the Langmuir isotherm. It is based on three key assumptions:

  1. All active sites are energetically identical (uniform surface).
  2. There are no interactions between adsorbed molecules.
  3. Adsorption is limited to a single monolayer.

Let Cv be the concentration of vacant sites, PA the partial pressure of A, and CA·S the concentration of adsorbed A. The net rate of adsorption is:

rAD = kA ( PACv  −  CA·S KA )

Where kA is the adsorption rate constant and KA = kA/k-A is the adsorption equilibrium constant. At equilibrium (rAD = 0), the fractional surface coverage (θA) is given by the Langmuir Isotherm:

θA = CA·S Ct = KA PA 1 + KA PA

*(Note: Ct is the total concentration of active sites, where Ct = Cv + CA·S)*

Step 4

Surface Reaction

The surface reaction is the actual chemical transformation. Depending on how molecules interact on the surface, we generally categorize the mechanisms into three types:

1. Single-Site Mechanism

An adsorbed reactant reacts to form an adsorbed product on the same site: A·S ⇌ B·S

The rate of surface reaction (rS) is:

rS = kS ( CA·S  −  CB·S KS )

2. Dual-Site (Langmuir-Hinshelwood) Mechanism

Two adsorbed molecules on adjacent sites react with one another. This is the most common mechanism for bimolecular surface reactions: A·S + B·S ⇌ C·S + D·S

The rate depends on the probability of A and B occupying adjacent sites, which is proportional to their respective surface concentrations:

rS = kS ( CA·S CB·S  −  CC·S CD·S KS )

3. Eley-Rideal Mechanism

An adsorbed molecule reacts directly with a molecule from the gas phase (no adsorption required for the second reactant): A·S + B(g) ⇌ C·S

The rate is proportional to the adsorbed concentration of A and the partial pressure of gas B:

rS = kS ( CA·S PB  −  CC·S KS )
Step 5

Desorption of Products

After the reaction, the product molecule (C) is still tethered to the active site (C·S). It must break this bond to free up the site for another catalytic cycle.

C·S ⇌ C + S

Desorption is simply the reverse of adsorption. The net rate of desorption (rD) can be written as:

rD = kD (CC·S − KD,rev PC Cv)

*(Where kD is the desorption rate constant and KD,rev is the reverse of the desorption equilibrium constant, which is mathematically identical to the adsorption equilibrium constant of C, KC)*

Langmuir Desorption Isotherm

If desorption is in equilibrium (i.e., it is not the rate-limiting step), we can set rD ≈ 0. The relationship dictates how product accumulation limits open sites. The equilibrium coverage of product C is:

θC = CC·S Ct = KC PC 1 + KA PA + KC PC

Crucial Consideration: If the product binds too strongly to the surface (KC is very large), it will not desorb. The active sites remain covered by the product, a phenomenon known as product inhibition or catalyst poisoning.

Step 6

Internal Diffusion of Products

Once the product has desorbed from the active site, it finds itself deep within the porous network of the catalyst pellet. It must now navigate out of the pore to the exterior surface.

Like reactant internal diffusion (Step 2), this process relies on concentration gradients. The concentration of product is highest deep inside the pore where the reaction occurred, driving diffusion outward.

Modes of Diffusion:

  • Bulk / Ordinary Diffusion: Occurs in large pores where product molecules collide more frequently with each other than with the pore walls.
  • Knudsen Diffusion: Occurs in very small pores (mesopores and micropores) where the mean free path of the molecule is larger than the pore diameter. Molecules collide primarily with the pore walls.

Note: In complex reactions involving multiple products, differences in internal diffusion rates between lighter and heavier products can significantly affect the final product distribution (selectivity) of the reactor.

Step 7

External Diffusion of Products

The final step. The product molecule has reached the external surface of the catalyst pellet and must now cross the hydrodynamic boundary layer to mix with the bulk fluid stream.

The flux of product C away from the surface, WC, is defined by the mass transfer coefficient for the product (kc,C) and the concentration gradient from the surface (CC,s) to the bulk (CC,b):

WC = kc,C (CC,s − CC,b)

Once in the bulk fluid, the product is swept away out of the reactor.

Completing the Cycle: With the product successfully transported away, the overall catalytic cycle for that specific reactant molecule is complete. In a steady-state system, all seven steps occur simultaneously and continuously at equal macroscopic rates.

Synthesis

Deriving Catalytic Rate Laws

To model a reactor, we need an equation that relates the rate of reaction to measurable bulk conditions (like pressure). We use the LHHW (Langmuir-Hinshelwood-Hougen-Watson) approach.

The Methodology:

  1. Assume a Rate-Limiting Step (RLS): Assume one step (usually adsorption, surface reaction, or desorption) is infinitely slow compared to the others.
  2. Assume Quasi-Equilibrium: Because the other steps are comparatively fast, assume they are at equilibrium. This allows us to use equilibrium constants (KA, KC) to express unmeasurable surface concentrations (CA·S) in terms of measurable gas partial pressures (PA).
  3. Site Balance: Apply a total site balance: Ct = Cv + CA·S + CB·S + … to eliminate the vacant site term Cv.
  4. Substitute and Simplify: Plug these expressions into the rate law of the RLS.

By following this procedure, almost all catalytic rate laws can be formatted into a universal structure:

−rA = (Kinetic Factor) × (Driving Force) (Adsorption Factor)n
  • Kinetic Factor: Contains the rate constant (k) of the rate-limiting step.
  • Driving Force: Represents the displacement from thermodynamic equilibrium (e.g., PAPB − PC/Keq).
  • Adsorption Factor: Represents the competition for active sites (e.g., 1 + KAPA + KCPC). n depends on the number of sites participating in the RLS.
Step 2

Internal Diffusion of Reactants

Most industrial catalysts are highly porous to maximize their surface area. Once the reactant reaches the external surface, it must diffuse through this tortuous pore network to reach the active sites deep inside the pellet.

Internal diffusion is characterized by an effective diffusivity ($D_{e}$), which accounts for the porosity ($\epsilon_p$), tortuosity ($\tau$), and constriction factor ($\sigma$) of the pores:

$$ D_e = \frac{D_{AB} \epsilon_p \sigma}{\tau} $$

The Thiele Modulus & Effectiveness Factor:

To quantify how much internal diffusion limits the reaction, chemical engineers use the Thiele Modulus ($\phi$), which is essentially the ratio of the surface reaction rate to the internal diffusion rate.

The Internal Effectiveness Factor ($\eta$) relates the actual overall rate to the rate that would occur if the entire interior were exposed to the external surface concentration:

$$ \eta = \frac{\text{Actual overall rate}}{\text{Rate evaluated at external surface conditions}} $$

For a first-order reaction in a spherical pellet, when internal diffusion is highly limiting (large $\phi$), $\eta \approx 3/\phi$.

Step 3

Adsorption of Reactants

Once inside the pore, the reactant molecule A must attach to an active site ($S$) on the catalyst surface to form an adsorbed complex ($A\cdot S$).

$$ A + S \rightleftharpoons A\cdot S $$

The Langmuir Adsorption Isotherm

The most common model to describe this is the Langmuir isotherm. It is based on three key assumptions:

  1. All active sites are energetically identical (uniform surface).
  2. There are no interactions between adsorbed molecules.
  3. Adsorption is limited to a single monolayer.

Let $C_v$ be the concentration of vacant sites, $P_A$ the partial pressure of A, and $C_{A\cdot S}$ the concentration of adsorbed A. The net rate of adsorption is:

$$ r_{AD} = k_A \left( P_A C_v - \frac{C_{A\cdot S}}{K_A} \right) $$

Where $k_A$ is the adsorption rate constant and $K_A = k_A/k_{-A}$ is the adsorption equilibrium constant. At equilibrium ($r_{AD} = 0$), the fractional surface coverage ($\theta_A$) is given by the Langmuir Isotherm:

$$ \theta_A = \frac{C_{A\cdot S}}{C_t} = \frac{K_A P_A}{1 + K_A P_A} $$

*(Note: $C_t$ is the total concentration of active sites, where $C_t = C_v + C_{A\cdot S}$)*

Step 4

Surface Reaction

The surface reaction is the actual chemical transformation. Depending on how molecules interact on the surface, we generally categorize the mechanisms into three types:

1. Single-Site Mechanism

An adsorbed reactant reacts to form an adsorbed product on the same site: $A\cdot S \rightleftharpoons B\cdot S$

The rate of surface reaction ($r_S$) is:

$$ r_S = k_S \left( C_{A\cdot S} - \frac{C_{B\cdot S}}{K_S} \right) $$

2. Dual-Site (Langmuir-Hinshelwood) Mechanism

Two adsorbed molecules on adjacent sites react with one another. This is the most common mechanism for bimolecular surface reactions: $A\cdot S + B\cdot S \rightleftharpoons C\cdot S + D\cdot S$

The rate depends on the probability of A and B occupying adjacent sites, which is proportional to their respective surface concentrations:

$$ r_S = k_S \left( C_{A\cdot S} C_{B\cdot S} - \frac{C_{C\cdot S} C_{D\cdot S}}{K_S} \right) $$

3. Eley-Rideal Mechanism

An adsorbed molecule reacts directly with a molecule from the gas phase (no adsorption required for the second reactant): $A\cdot S + B(g) \rightleftharpoons C\cdot S$

The rate is proportional to the adsorbed concentration of A and the partial pressure of gas B:

$$ r_S = k_S \left( C_{A\cdot S} P_B - \frac{C_{C\cdot S}}{K_S} \right) $$
Step 5

Desorption of Products

After the reaction, the product molecule ($C$) is still tethered to the active site ($C\cdot S$). It must break this bond to free up the site for another catalytic cycle.

$$ C\cdot S \rightleftharpoons C + S $$

Desorption is simply the reverse of adsorption. The net rate of desorption ($r_D$) can be written as:

$$ r_D = k_D \left( C_{C\cdot S} - K_{D,rev} P_C C_v \right) $$

*(Where $k_D$ is the desorption rate constant and $K_{D,rev}$ is the reverse of the desorption equilibrium constant, which is mathematically identical to the adsorption equilibrium constant of C, $K_C$)*

Langmuir Desorption Isotherm

If desorption is in equilibrium (i.e., it is not the rate-limiting step), we can set $r_D \approx 0$. The relationship dictates how product accumulation limits open sites. The equilibrium coverage of product C is:

$$ \theta_C = \frac{C_{C\cdot S}}{C_t} = \frac{K_C P_C}{1 + K_A P_A + K_C P_C} $$

Crucial Consideration: If the product binds too strongly to the surface ($K_C$ is very large), it will not desorb. The active sites remain covered by the product, a phenomenon known as product inhibition or catalyst poisoning.

Step 6

Internal Diffusion of Products

Once the product has desorbed from the active site, it finds itself deep within the porous network of the catalyst pellet. It must now navigate out of the pore to the exterior surface.

Like reactant internal diffusion (Step 2), this process relies on concentration gradients. The concentration of product is highest deep inside the pore where the reaction occurred, driving diffusion outward.

Modes of Diffusion:

  • Bulk / Ordinary Diffusion: Occurs in large pores where product molecules collide more frequently with each other than with the pore walls.
  • Knudsen Diffusion: Occurs in very small pores (mesopores and micropores) where the mean free path of the molecule is larger than the pore diameter. Molecules collide primarily with the pore walls.

Note: In complex reactions involving multiple products, differences in internal diffusion rates between lighter and heavier products can significantly affect the final product distribution (selectivity) of the reactor.

Step 7

External Diffusion of Products

The final step. The product molecule has reached the external surface of the catalyst pellet and must now cross the hydrodynamic boundary layer to mix with the bulk fluid stream.

The flux of product C away from the surface, $W_C$, is defined by the mass transfer coefficient for the product ($k_{c,C}$) and the concentration gradient from the surface ($C_{C,s}$) to the bulk ($C_{C,b}$):

$$ W_C = k_{c,C} (C_{C,s} - C_{C,b}) $$

Once in the bulk fluid, the product is swept away out of the reactor.

Completing the Cycle: With the product successfully transported away, the overall catalytic cycle for that specific reactant molecule is complete. In a steady-state system, all seven steps occur simultaneously and continuously at equal macroscopic rates.

Synthesis

Deriving Catalytic Rate Laws

To model a reactor, we need an equation that relates the rate of reaction to measurable bulk conditions (like pressure). We use the LHHW (Langmuir-Hinshelwood-Hougen-Watson) approach.

The Methodology:

  1. Assume a Rate-Limiting Step (RLS): Assume one step (usually adsorption, surface reaction, or desorption) is infinitely slow compared to the others.
  2. Assume Quasi-Equilibrium: Because the other steps are comparatively fast, assume they are at equilibrium. This allows us to use equilibrium constants ($K_A$, $K_C$) to express unmeasurable surface concentrations ($C_{A\cdot S}$) in terms of measurable gas partial pressures ($P_A$).
  3. Site Balance: Apply a total site balance: $C_t = C_v + C_{A\cdot S} + C_{B\cdot S} + \dots$ to eliminate the vacant site term $C_v$.
  4. Substitute and Simplify: Plug these expressions into the rate law of the RLS.

By following this procedure, almost all catalytic rate laws can be formatted into a universal structure:

$$ -r_A = \frac{(\text{Kinetic Factor}) \times (\text{Driving Force})}{(\text{Adsorption Factor})^n} $$
  • Kinetic Factor: Contains the rate constant ($k$) of the rate-limiting step.
  • Driving Force: Represents the displacement from thermodynamic equilibrium (e.g., $P_A P_B - P_C/K_{eq}$).
  • Adsorption Factor: Represents the competition for active sites (e.g., $1 + K_A P_A + K_C P_C$). $n$ depends on the number of sites participating in the RLS.
